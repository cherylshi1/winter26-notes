1. Schrödinger Equation Foundations

The Two Schrödinger Equations

Everything in this course flows from these two equations. Know when to use each one.

Time-Independent (TISE) — used most of the time
$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + U(x)\psi(x) = E\,\psi(x)$$

Use when: $U(x)$ doesn't depend on time. Gives you the allowed energies $E_n$ and spatial wavefunctions $\psi_n(x)$.

Time-Dependent (TDSE) — the full deal
$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial x^2} + U(x)\Psi = i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t}$$

Use when: you need time evolution, superposition states, or probability that changes with time.

Key relationship between them

If you solve the TISE and get $\psi_n(x)$ with energy $E_n$, the full time-dependent solution is:

$$\Psi_n(x,t) = \psi_n(x)\,e^{-i\omega_n t}, \quad \omega_n = \frac{E_n}{\hbar}$$

These are stationary states: $|\Psi_n|^2 = |\psi_n|^2$ — the probability doesn't change in time.

Probability & Normalization

$$P(a \le x \le b) = \int_a^b |\psi(x)|^2\,dx, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi(x)|^2\,dx = 1$$

Uncertainty Principle

$$\Delta x\,\Delta p \gtrsim \hbar$$

If $\Delta x$ is small (particle well-localized), then $\Delta p$ must be large (momentum very uncertain), and vice versa. This is why the infinite square well ground state has nonzero energy — confining the particle to width $a$ forces a minimum momentum, hence minimum kinetic energy.

Key Constants & Relations

2. Bound States & Sketching Wavefunctions

The Curvature Equation

$$\psi''(x) = \frac{2m}{\hbar^2}\bigl[U(x) - E\bigr]\,\psi(x)$$
RegionConditionSign of $\psi''$Behavior
Classically allowed$E > U(x)$Opposite to $\psi$$\psi$ curves toward the $x$-axis → oscillatory
Classically forbidden$E < U(x)$Same sign as $\psi$$\psi$ curves away from the $x$-axis → exponential
Fig 2.1 — Curvature rules for sketching wavefunctions. Left: in the classically allowed region, ψ oscillates (bends toward axis). Right: in the forbidden region, ψ decays or grows exponentially (bends away from axis). Only the decaying solution is physical.
Sketching Rules (Quiz 1 type!)

1. Draw horizontal line for $E$ on the $U(x)$ plot. Identify classical turning points where $E = U(x)$.

2. Inside the well ($E > U$): $\psi$ oscillates. More curvature where $E - U$ is larger (shorter wavelength).

3. Outside the well ($E < U$): $\psi$ decays exponentially. Faster decay where $U - E$ is larger.

4. $\psi$ and $\psi'$ are always continuous (smooth) at transitions (except $\psi'$ can be discontinuous at infinite walls).

5. Ground state: 0 nodes. First excited: 1 node. The $n$-th state has $n-1$ nodes.

6. At infinite walls, $\psi = 0$. At finite walls, $\psi$ smoothly decays into the forbidden region.

Infinite Square Well

Potential
$$U(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \le x \le a \\ \infty & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
Solutions
$$\psi_n = \sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right), \quad E_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2}$$
Fig 2.2 — Infinite square well: first three wavefunctions with energy levels. Note ψ = 0 at both walls and the number of nodes increases with n.

Finite Square Well

Key differences from infinite well: wavefunctions leak into the forbidden region (exponential tails), energies are lower, and there are only finitely many bound states.

3. Simple Harmonic Oscillator (SHO)

Why the SHO matters so much

Any potential near a minimum can be approximated as $U(x) \approx \frac{1}{2}kx^2$ for small oscillations. This makes the SHO the universal model for vibrations — diatomic molecules, crystal lattices, quantum fields. It appeared on the 2024 final, 2025 final (as 2D SHO), and Quiz 1.

Setup & Solution

$$U(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2, \qquad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$
Energy levels (equally spaced!)
$$\boxed{E_n = \left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$
Ground state wavefunction
$$\boxed{\psi_0(x) = A_0\,e^{-x^2/2b^2}}, \quad b^2 = \frac{\hbar}{m\omega} = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{mk}}$$
Fig 3.1 — SHO: parabolic potential with equally spaced energy levels and wavefunctions. The Gaussian ground state has no nodes; each higher state adds one node. Wavefunctions extend slightly past the classical turning points (tunneling).

Normalization of ground state: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|A_0|^2 e^{-x^2/b^2}dx = 1$. Using $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-\lambda x^2}dx = \sqrt{\pi/\lambda}$ with $\lambda = 1/b^2$:

$$|A_0|^2 \sqrt{\pi}\,b = 1 \implies A_0 = \frac{1}{(\pi b^2)^{1/4}}$$

Key features: $E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega \neq 0$ (zero-point energy from uncertainty principle). Energy levels equally spaced by $\hbar\omega$ — unique to harmonic potential. Hermite polynomials: $\psi_n \propto P_n(x/b)\,e^{-x^2/2b^2}$. Orthogonality: $\int \psi_n^* \psi_m\,dx = 0$ for $n \neq m$.

2025 Final Q1 — 2D SHO (33 pts)

Asked about $U(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 + \frac{1}{2}k'y^2$: write 2D Schrödinger, separate variables, verify Gaussian solution, find energy levels and degeneracies when $k' = 4k$. See Section 6.

4. Time-Dependent Schrödinger & Superposition

Stationary States vs. Superposition

A single eigenstate $\Psi_n(x,t) = \psi_n(x)e^{-i\omega_n t}$ has $|\Psi_n|^2 = |\psi_n|^2$ — time-independent. But a superposition:

$$\Psi(x,t) = a\,\psi_1(x)\,e^{-i\omega_1 t} + b\,\psi_2(x)\,e^{-i\omega_2 t}$$
Probability density of a superposition
$$|\Psi(x,t)|^2 = a^2|\psi_1|^2 + b^2|\psi_2|^2 + 2ab\,\psi_1(x)\psi_2(x)\cos(\omega_{21}t)$$

where $\omega_{21} = \omega_2 - \omega_1 = (E_2 - E_1)/\hbar$. The cross-term oscillates — this is how probability "sloshes" back and forth.

Fig 4.1 — A superposition of ψ₁ and ψ₂ in an infinite well "sloshes" back and forth at the beat frequency ω₂₁. The probability density |Ψ|² oscillates in time, unlike a single stationary state.

Normalization requires: $|a|^2 + |b|^2 = 1$. Derivation trick: $|u + ve^{i\theta}|^2 = u^2 + v^2 + 2uv\cos\theta$.

5. Scattering & Tunneling

Step Potential: E > U₀

Fig 5.1 — Step potential with E > U₀. The incident wave (blue) partially reflects (orange, dashed) and partially transmits (green). The transmitted wave has a longer wavelength because the particle has less kinetic energy in Region II.
Reflection coefficient
$$\boxed{R = \left(\frac{k_1 - k_2}{k_1 + k_2}\right)^2}$$
Transmission coefficient
$$\boxed{T = \frac{k_2}{k_1}\frac{|B|^2}{|A_0|^2} = \frac{4k_1 k_2}{(k_1 + k_2)^2}}$$
Why the $k_2/k_1$ factor in $T$?

The particle moves at different speeds in the two regions ($v \propto k$). The $k_2/k_1$ ratio accounts for the probability current — the rate particles flow past a point. Always: $R + T = 1$.

Tunneling Through a Finite Barrier

Fig 5.2 — Tunneling through a barrier. The wavefunction decays exponentially inside the barrier but emerges on the other side with reduced amplitude. The transmitted wave has the same wavelength as the incident wave (same energy, same U = 0).
$$\boxed{T \approx 16\frac{E}{U_0}\left(1 - \frac{E}{U_0}\right)e^{-2\alpha L}}, \qquad \alpha = \frac{\sqrt{2m(U_0 - E)}}{\hbar}$$

The exponential $e^{-2\alpha L}$ dominates: tunneling probability drops off exponentially with barrier width $L$ and with $\sqrt{U_0 - E}$.

6. Higher Dimensions: 2D & 3D Boxes

Separation of Variables — The Master Technique

Separation of Variables Recipe

1. Write the multi-dimensional Schrödinger equation.

2. Guess a product: $\psi(x,y) = X(x)Y(y)$.

3. Substitute and divide by $XY$: $\frac{X''}{X} + \frac{Y''}{Y} = \text{const}$.

4. Each term depends on only one variable → each must separately equal a constant.

5. Solve two independent ODEs. Total energy: $E = E_x + E_y$.

2D Infinite Square Box

$$\psi_{n_x, n_y} = A\sin\!\left(\frac{n_x \pi x}{a}\right)\sin\!\left(\frac{n_y \pi y}{b}\right), \qquad \boxed{E = \frac{\hbar^2\pi^2}{2m}\left(\frac{n_x^2}{a^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{b^2}\right)}$$

Degeneracy in a Square Box ($a = b$)

Fig 6.1 — Energy levels of a 2D square box. When different (n_x, n_y) combinations give the same n²_x + n²_y, the states are degenerate. Same energy, different spatial patterns.

2D SHO

$$\boxed{E_{n_x, n_y} = \left(n_x + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega_x + \left(n_y + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega_y}$$

8. The Hydrogen Atom

2025 Final Q2 — Hydrogen atom (34 pts)

Asked: radial probability $P(r)$ for ground state, probability inside $a_B$, expectation value $\langle r \rangle$. Make sure you can do these integrals.

Energy Levels & Quantum Numbers

$$\boxed{E_n = -\frac{E_R}{n^2}}, \quad E_R = 13.6\;\text{eV}, \qquad a_B = \frac{\hbar^2}{m_e k_e e^2} \approx 0.529\;\text{Å}$$
Fig 8.1 — Hydrogen energy levels. E_n = -13.6/n² eV. In hydrogen, all l states at the same n are degenerate (same energy). Transitions between levels emit/absorb photons (Lyman, Balmer, Paschen series).

Radial Probability Distribution

$$\boxed{P(r) = r^2|R_{nl}(r)|^2}$$

For the ground state: $P_{1s}(r) = \frac{4}{a_B^3}\,r^2\,e^{-2r/a_B}$. Most probable radius at $r = a_B$, average $\langle r \rangle = \frac{3}{2}a_B$.

Fig 8.2 — Radial probability P(r) for the 1s, 2s, and 2p states. The 1s peaks at r = a_B. The 2s has two peaks (inner shell penetration). The 2p starts at zero (l ≠ 0 → no probability at nucleus) and peaks at 4a_B.

Key Wavefunctions & Results

Ground state (1s): $\psi_{1s} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}\,a_B^{3/2}}\,e^{-r/a_B}$ — spherically symmetric.

2s: $R_{2s} \propto (2 - r/a_B)e^{-r/2a_B}$ — node at $r = 2a_B$.

2p: $R_{2p} \propto r\,e^{-r/2a_B}$ — zero at origin, peak at $4a_B$.

Only s-states (l = 0) have nonzero probability at r = 0

$R_{nl}(r) \propto r^l$ near $r = 0$. This means s-electrons penetrate closest to the nucleus → they're more tightly bound in multi-electron atoms. This breaks the hydrogen degeneracy.

Expectation values: $\langle r \rangle_{1s} = \frac{3}{2}a_B$, $\langle U \rangle_{1s} = -k_e e^2/a_B = -2E_R$. Useful integral: $\int_0^{\infty}r^n e^{-r/b}dr = n!\,b^{n+1}$.

Hydrogen-like Atoms

$$E_n = -\frac{Z^2 E_R}{n^2}, \qquad a_Z = \frac{a_B}{Z}$$

9. Electron Spin & Multi-electron Atoms

2025 Final Q3 — Multi-electron atoms (33 pts)

Electron configurations of Li, F, C; first excited state of Ne.

Electron Spin

$s = \frac{1}{2}$ always, $m_s = \pm\frac{1}{2}$, $|\vec{S}| = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\hbar$. Each orbital holds 2 electrons (Pauli exclusion: no two electrons share all four quantum numbers $n, l, m_l, m_s$).

Energy Level Ordering in Multi-electron Atoms

Fig 9.1 — Energy level ordering in multi-electron atoms. Unlike hydrogen, energy depends on BOTH n and l. The numbered circles show filling order. Key exam point: 4s fills before 3d because s-electrons penetrate closer to the nucleus.

10. Master Formula Map & Decision Tree

Decision Tree: What Formula Do I Use?

Step 1: What kind of potential?

→ Infinite well → $\psi_n = \sqrt{2/a}\sin(n\pi x/a)$, $E_n = n^2 E_0$

→ Finite well → curvature rules to sketch; exponential decay outside

→ Harmonic ($\frac{1}{2}kx^2$) → $E_n = (n+\frac{1}{2})\hbar\omega$, ground state Gaussian

→ Step or barrier → scattering/tunneling (match boundary conditions)

→ Coulomb ($-ke^2/r$) → hydrogen: $E_n = -E_R/n^2$

→ Something weird → sketch using curvature rules; maybe approximate as SHO near minimum

Step 2: How many dimensions?

→ 1D: solve directly

→ Multi-D with $U = U_x + U_y$: separate in Cartesian, $E = E_x + E_y$

→ Multi-D with $U = U(r)$: separate in polar/spherical

Step 3: What are they asking?

→ Energy levels → eigenvalue equation   → Probability → integrate $|\psi|^2$

→ Expectation value → $\langle A \rangle = \int \psi^* A \psi\,dx$   → Degeneracy → count states with same $E$

Common Exam Traps

Watch out for these

1. $T \neq |B/A_0|^2$ — don't forget the $k_2/k_1$ factor in transmission.

2. $|\vec{L}| = \sqrt{l(l+1)}\hbar$, NOT $l\hbar$.

3. $l$ goes from $0$ to $n-1$, not $n$.

4. Radial probability is $P(r) = r^2|R|^2$, not $|R|^2$.

5. 4s fills before 3d in multi-electron atoms.

6. Dimensions of $\psi$: 1D: $1/\sqrt{\text{m}}$; 3D: $1/\text{m}^{3/2}$.

7. Degeneracy: Don't forget spin (×2) when counting electron states.
