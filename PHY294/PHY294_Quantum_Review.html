1. Schrödinger Equation Foundations

The Two Schrödinger Equations

Everything in this course flows from these two equations. Know when to use each one.

Time-Independent (TISE) — used most of the time
$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + U(x)\psi(x) = E\,\psi(x)$$

Use when: $U(x)$ doesn't depend on time. Gives you the allowed energies $E_n$ and spatial wavefunctions $\psi_n(x)$.

Time-Dependent (TDSE) — the full deal
$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial x^2} + U(x)\Psi = i\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t}$$

Use when: you need time evolution, superposition states, or probability that changes with time.

Key relationship between them MUST KNOW

If you solve the TISE and get $\psi_n(x)$ with energy $E_n$, the full time-dependent solution is:

$$\Psi_n(x,t) = \psi_n(x)\,e^{-i\omega_n t}, \quad \omega_n = \frac{E_n}{\hbar}$$

These are stationary states: $|\Psi_n|^2 = |\psi_n|^2$ — the probability doesn't change in time.

Probability & Normalization

$$P(a \le x \le b) = \int_a^b |\psi(x)|^2\,dx, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi(x)|^2\,dx = 1$$

Uncertainty Principle

$$\Delta x\,\Delta p \gtrsim \hbar$$

If $\Delta x$ is small (particle well-localized), then $\Delta p$ must be large (momentum very uncertain), and vice versa. This is why the infinite square well ground state has nonzero energy — confining the particle to width $a$ forces a minimum momentum, hence minimum kinetic energy.

3. Simple Harmonic Oscillator (SHO)

Why the SHO matters so much GOOD TO KNOW

Any potential near a minimum can be approximated as $U(x) \approx \frac{1}{2}kx^2$ for small oscillations. This makes the SHO the universal model for vibrations — diatomic molecules, crystal lattices, quantum fields. It appeared on the 2024 final, 2025 final (as 2D SHO), and Quiz 1.

Setup & Solution

$$U(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2, \qquad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$
Energy levels (equally spaced!)
$$\boxed{E_n = \left(n + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$
Ground state wavefunction
$$\boxed{\psi_0(x) = A_0\,e^{-x^2/2b^2}}, \quad b^2 = \frac{\hbar}{m\omega} = \frac{\hbar}{\sqrt{mk}}$$
Fig 3.1 — SHO: parabolic potential with equally spaced energy levels and wavefunctions. The Gaussian ground state has no nodes; each higher state adds one node. Wavefunctions extend slightly past the classical turning points (tunneling).

Normalization of ground state: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|A_0|^2 e^{-x^2/b^2}dx = 1$. Using $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}e^{-\lambda x^2}dx = \sqrt{\pi/\lambda}$ with $\lambda = 1/b^2$:

$$|A_0|^2 \sqrt{\pi}\,b = 1 \implies A_0 = \frac{1}{(\pi b^2)^{1/4}}$$

Key features: $E_0 = \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega \neq 0$ (zero-point energy from uncertainty principle). Energy levels equally spaced by $\hbar\omega$ — unique to harmonic potential. Hermite polynomials: $\psi_n \propto P_n(x/b)\,e^{-x^2/2b^2}$. Orthogonality: $\int \psi_n^* \psi_m\,dx = 0$ for $n \neq m$.

2025 Final Q1 — 2D SHO (33 pts) MUST KNOW

Asked about $U(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 + \frac{1}{2}k'y^2$: write 2D Schrödinger, separate variables, verify Gaussian solution, find energy levels and degeneracies when $k' = 4k$. See Section 6.

4. Time-Dependent Schrödinger & Superposition

Stationary States vs. Superposition

A single eigenstate $\Psi_n(x,t) = \psi_n(x)e^{-i\omega_n t}$ has $|\Psi_n|^2 = |\psi_n|^2$ — time-independent. But a superposition:

$$\Psi(x,t) = a\,\psi_1(x)\,e^{-i\omega_1 t} + b\,\psi_2(x)\,e^{-i\omega_2 t}$$
Probability density of a superposition GOOD TO KNOW
$$|\Psi(x,t)|^2 = a^2|\psi_1|^2 + b^2|\psi_2|^2 + 2ab\,\psi_1(x)\psi_2(x)\cos(\omega_{21}t)$$

where $\omega_{21} = \omega_2 - \omega_1 = (E_2 - E_1)/\hbar$. The cross-term oscillates — this is how probability "sloshes" back and forth.

Fig 4.1 — A superposition of ψ₁ and ψ₂ in an infinite well "sloshes" back and forth at the beat frequency ω₂₁. The probability density |Ψ|² oscillates in time, unlike a single stationary state.

Normalization requires: $|a|^2 + |b|^2 = 1$. Derivation trick: $|u + ve^{i\theta}|^2 = u^2 + v^2 + 2uv\cos\theta$.

5. Scattering & Tunneling

Step Potential: E > U₀

Fig 5.1 — Step potential with E > U₀. The incident wave (blue) partially reflects (orange, dashed) and partially transmits (green). The transmitted wave has a longer wavelength because the particle has less kinetic energy in Region II.
Reflection coefficient
$$\boxed{R = \left(\frac{k_1 - k_2}{k_1 + k_2}\right)^2}$$
Transmission coefficient
$$\boxed{T = \frac{k_2}{k_1}\frac{|B|^2}{|A_0|^2} = \frac{4k_1 k_2}{(k_1 + k_2)^2}}$$
Why the $k_2/k_1$ factor in $T$? GOOD TO KNOW

The particle moves at different speeds in the two regions ($v \propto k$). The $k_2/k_1$ ratio accounts for the probability current — the rate particles flow past a point. Always: $R + T = 1$.

Tunneling Through a Finite Barrier

Fig 5.2 — Tunneling through a barrier. The wavefunction decays exponentially inside the barrier but emerges on the other side with reduced amplitude. The transmitted wave has the same wavelength as the incident wave (same energy, same U = 0).
$$\boxed{T \approx 16\frac{E}{U_0}\left(1 - \frac{E}{U_0}\right)e^{-2\alpha L}}, \qquad \alpha = \frac{\sqrt{2m(U_0 - E)}}{\hbar}$$

The exponential $e^{-2\alpha L}$ dominates: tunneling probability drops off exponentially with barrier width $L$ and with $\sqrt{U_0 - E}$.

6. Higher Dimensions: 2D & 3D Boxes

Separation of Variables — The Master Technique

Separation of Variables Recipe MUST KNOW

1. Write the multi-dimensional Schrödinger equation.

2. Guess a product: $\psi(x,y) = X(x)Y(y)$.

3. Substitute and divide by $XY$: $\frac{X''}{X} + \frac{Y''}{Y} = \text{const}$.

4. Each term depends on only one variable → each must separately equal a constant.

5. Solve two independent ODEs. Total energy: $E = E_x + E_y$.

Worked Example: 2D SHO (2025 Final Q1 style)

Problem: Given $U(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 + \frac{1}{2}k'y^2$, find the energy levels.

Step 1 — Write the 2D Schrödinger equation MUST KNOW

Start from the general 2D TISE: $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\left(\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial y^2}\right) + \left(\tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}k'y^2\right)\psi = E\,\psi$$

Key observation: The potential is a sum of an x-only part and a y-only part: $U(x,y) = U_x(x) + U_y(y)$. This is the signal that separation of variables will work!

Step 2 — Guess a product solution MUST KNOW

Assume $\psi(x,y) = X(x)\,Y(y)$. Substitute into the Schrödinger equation:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\left(X''Y + XY''\right) + \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 XY + \tfrac{1}{2}k'y^2 XY = E\,XY$$
Step 3 — Divide through by $XY$ MUST KNOW

Divide every term by $X(x)Y(y)$:

$$\underbrace{-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{X''}{X} + \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2}_{\text{depends only on } x} \;+\; \underbrace{-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{Y''}{Y} + \tfrac{1}{2}k'y^2}_{\text{depends only on } y} \;=\; E$$
Step 4 — The Separation Argument (this is the key logic!) MUST KNOW

The left side has [function of $x$ only] + [function of $y$ only] = constant. If you change $x$ while holding $y$ fixed, the $x$-part changes but the $y$-part doesn't — yet the sum must still equal $E$. The only way this works is if each part is individually constant:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{X''}{X} + \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = E_x \qquad \text{and} \qquad -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{Y''}{Y} + \tfrac{1}{2}k'y^2 = E_y$$

where $E_x + E_y = E$. We now have two independent 1D problems.

Step 5 — Solve each 1D problem (we already know the answer!) MUST KNOW

Each equation is just a 1D SHO! From Section 3, the 1D SHO with spring constant $k$ has $E_n = (n + \frac{1}{2})\hbar\omega$ where $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$. So:

$$E_x = \left(n_x + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega_x, \quad \omega_x = \sqrt{k/m} \qquad\qquad E_y = \left(n_y + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega_y, \quad \omega_y = \sqrt{k'/m}$$

Total energy:

$$\boxed{E_{n_x,n_y} = \left(n_x + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega_x + \left(n_y + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega_y} \qquad n_x, n_y = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

Total wavefunction: $\psi_{n_x,n_y}(x,y) = X_{n_x}(x) \cdot Y_{n_y}(y)$ — product of two 1D SHO wavefunctions. For the ground state $(0,0)$: $\psi_{0,0} = A\,e^{-x^2/2b_x^2}\,e^{-y^2/2b_y^2}$ where $b_x = \sqrt{\hbar/m\omega_x}$, $b_y = \sqrt{\hbar/m\omega_y}$.

When does separation of variables work? GOOD TO KNOW

It works when $U(x,y) = f(x) + g(y)$ — the potential splits into independent parts. It does NOT work if there's a cross-term like $U = kxy$. Also works in polar/spherical when $U = U(r)$ only (central force → Section 7).

Same method, different potentials

The separation technique is identical for every 2D/3D problem — only Step 5 changes depending on what 1D problem you get:

• $U = 0$ inside a box → 1D infinite wells → $\sin$ solutions
• $U = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 + \frac{1}{2}k'y^2$ → 1D SHOs → Gaussian × Hermite solutions
• $U = U(r)$ in spherical coords → radial equation + angular equation → $R_{nl}(r) \cdot Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$

7. Central Forces & Angular Momentum

This section builds the framework for hydrogen CONTEXT

The central force separation gives you quantum numbers $l$ and $m$, the angular functions, and quantized angular momentum — all needed for hydrogen.

Separation in Spherical Coordinates

Why spherical? When $U(\vec{r}) = U(r)$ (depends only on distance from origin), Cartesian coordinates won't separate — you can't write $U(r) = f(x) + g(y) + h(z)$. But in spherical coordinates $(r, \theta, \phi)$, the potential only appears in the radial part, so the angular equations separate cleanly.

Step 1 — Write the Schrödinger equation in spherical coordinates MUST KNOW

The Laplacian $\nabla^2$ in spherical coordinates is messy (you don't need to memorize it), but the key point is:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi + U(r)\psi = E\psi$$

where $\nabla^2$ has a radial part (involving $\frac{\partial}{\partial r}$) and an angular part (involving $\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}$ and $\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}$).

Step 2 — Guess $\psi(r,\theta,\phi) = R(r)\,\Theta(\theta)\,\Phi(\phi)$ MUST KNOW

Substitute and divide by $R\Theta\Phi$. Because $U(r)$ depends only on $r$, the equation separates into:

• A radial equation for $R(r)$ — contains $U(r)$ and determines the energy.

• An angular equation which further separates into $\Theta(\theta)$ and $\Phi(\phi)$ — these are the same for every central force (they don't depend on $U(r)$ at all!).

Step 3 — Solve the $\Phi$ equation (easiest) MUST KNOW
$$\frac{d^2\Phi}{d\phi^2} = -m_l^2\,\Phi \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad \Phi(\phi) = e^{im_l\phi}$$

Boundary condition: $\Phi(\phi + 2\pi) = \Phi(\phi)$ (going around a full circle must return to the same value). This requires $e^{im_l \cdot 2\pi} = 1$, which forces $m_l$ to be an integer: $m_l = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots$

This is where the magnetic quantum number $m_l$ comes from — it's the quantization of $L_z = m_l\hbar$.

Step 4 — Solve the $\Theta$ equation MUST KNOW

This gives the associated Legendre equation. The solutions are the associated Legendre polynomials $\Theta_{l,m}(\theta)$. The requirement that $\Theta$ stays finite at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi$ (the poles) forces:

• $l = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ (orbital angular momentum quantum number)

• $|m_l| \le l$ (so for a given $l$, there are $2l + 1$ allowed values of $m_l$)

Together, $\Theta_{l,m}(\theta)\,\Phi_{m}(\phi) = Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$ are the spherical harmonics — the angular part of the wavefunction. These are universal for all central-force problems.

The first few angular functions $\Theta_{l,m}(\theta)$ (note: $\Theta_{l,-m} = (-1)^m \Theta_{l,m}$):

Step 5 — Solve the radial equation (depends on the specific $U(r)$) MUST KNOW

The radial equation contains $U(r)$ and a centrifugal term $\frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2mr^2}$:

$$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d}{dr}\!\left(r^2\frac{dR}{dr}\right) + \left[U(r) + \frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2mr^2}\right]R = E\,R$$

For hydrogen, $U(r) = -k_e e^2/r$, and solving gives $R_{nl}(r)$ with the constraint $l < n$ and energies $E_n = -13.6/n^2$ eV.

How To Approach Central-Force Questions

Five-step workflow (use this every time) MUST KNOW

1. Recognize the setup: if $U(\vec{r}) = U(r)$ only, or you see hydrogen / orbital labels / quantum numbers $n,l,m$ / angular functions like $\cos\theta$ or $e^{im\phi}$ → use spherical coordinates.

2. Read $m$ from the $\phi$ dependence (see pattern-matching table below).

3. Read $l$ from the $\theta$ shape or orbital label ($s \to 0$, $p \to 1$, $d \to 2$). Must satisfy $l \ge |m|$.

4. Read $n$ from the hydrogen label or radial factor.

5. Compute what they ask: $|L| = \sqrt{l(l+1)}\hbar$, $L_z = m\hbar$, degeneracy $= 2l+1$, nodes = ($n-l-1$ radial, $l$ angular).

Reading $l$ and $m$ — Pattern-Matching Table

How to read $m$

If the angular part contains $e^{im\phi}$, then the value of $m$ is just the number multiplying $\phi$.

$e^{i\phi} \Rightarrow m=+1$

$e^{-i\phi} \Rightarrow m=-1$

$e^{2i\phi} \Rightarrow m=+2$

No $\phi$ dependence $\Rightarrow m=0$

How to read $l$

Match the $\theta$-shape to the first few spherical harmonics:

constant $\Rightarrow l=0$

$\cos\theta$ or $\sin\theta$-type shape $\Rightarrow l=1$

$3\cos^2\theta-1$, $\sin\theta\cos\theta$, or $\sin^2\theta$-type shape $\Rightarrow l=2$

Very important: real orbitals often have definite $l$ but NOT definite $m$ GOOD TO KNOW

If you see $\cos\phi$ or $\sin\phi$ instead of $e^{im\phi}$, rewrite them using exponentials:

$$\cos\phi = \frac{e^{i\phi}+e^{-i\phi}}{2}, \qquad \sin\phi = \frac{e^{i\phi}-e^{-i\phi}}{2i}$$

That means a real orbital like $p_x \propto \sin\theta\cos\phi$ is a combination of $m=+1$ and $m=-1$. So it has definite $l=1$, but it is not an eigenstate of $L_z$.

Worked Examples: How To Use $n$, $l$, and $m$

Example 1 — Identify the state and angular momentum from a wavefunction MUST KNOW

Suppose $\psi(r,\theta,\phi) \propto r\,e^{-r/2a_B}\cos\theta$.

Step 1: There is no $\phi$ dependence, so $\Phi = 1$ and therefore $m = 0$.

Step 2: The angular factor is $\cos\theta$, which matches the $l=1,\;m=0$ spherical harmonic. So $l = 1$.

Step 3: The radial factor looks like $r\,e^{-r/2a_B}$, which is the hydrogen $2p$ radial behavior. So $n = 2$.

Conclusion: This is the $2p_z$ state.

Now use the formulas: $|L| = \sqrt{1(1+1)}\hbar = \sqrt{2}\hbar$, and $L_z = 0$.

Node check: radial nodes $= n-l-1 = 2-1-1 = 0$, angular nodes $= l = 1$.

Example 2 — What does the label "3d" tell you immediately? MUST KNOW

The label $3d$ means $n = 3$ and $l = 2$.

If $m$ is not specified: the possible values are $m = -2,-1,0,+1,+2$.

Total angular momentum: every one of these states has $|L| = \sqrt{2(2+1)}\hbar = \sqrt{6}\hbar$.

z-component: depends on which $m$ you choose, so $L_z = m\hbar$ can be $-2\hbar,-\hbar,0,+\hbar,+2\hbar$.

Degeneracy from orientation: $2l+1 = 5$ spatial states. Including spin, that becomes $10$ electron states.

Hydrogen node count: radial nodes $= n-l-1 = 0$, angular nodes $= l = 2$.

Example 3 — Read $m$ directly from the $\phi$ dependence MUST KNOW

Suppose $\psi(r,\theta,\phi) \propto R(r)\,\sin^2\theta\,e^{-2i\phi}$.

Step 1: The factor $e^{-2i\phi}$ tells you immediately that $m=-2$.

Step 2: Since $|m|=2$, the orbital number must satisfy $l \ge 2$.

Step 3: The $\theta$ dependence is $\sin^2\theta$, which matches the $l=2$ pattern.

Conclusion: $l=2$, $m=-2$.

Angular momentum: $|L|=\sqrt{6}\hbar$, $L_z=-2\hbar$.

Example 4 — Recognize a real orbital: $p_x$ MUST KNOW

Suppose the angular part is $\sin\theta\cos\phi$.

Step 1: The $\sin\theta$ shape says this is a $p$-type angular shape, so $l=1$.

Step 2: But $\cos\phi$ is not a single exponential. Rewrite it: $$\sin\theta\cos\phi = \frac{1}{2}\sin\theta e^{i\phi} + \frac{1}{2}\sin\theta e^{-i\phi}$$

So this is a combination of $m=+1$ and $m=-1$ states.

Conclusion: definite $l=1$, but not definite $m$.

Exam meaning: if they ask for $|L|$, you can still say $|L|=\sqrt{2}\hbar$. But you should not give a single value of $L_z$.

Example 5 — Recognize a real orbital: $d_{x^2-y^2}$ MUST KNOW

Suppose the angular part is $\sin^2\theta\cos 2\phi$.

Step 1: The $\sin^2\theta$ pattern is a $d$-type shape, so $l=2$.

Step 2: Rewrite the $\phi$ dependence: $$\cos 2\phi = \frac{e^{2i\phi}+e^{-2i\phi}}{2}$$

So this state is a combination of $m=+2$ and $m=-2$.

Conclusion: definite $l=2$, but not definite $m$.

Angular momentum: $|L|=\sqrt{6}\hbar$, but there is no single $L_z$ value.

Example 6 — Quick exam decoding MUST KNOW

If $\psi \propto R_{31}(r)\sin\theta\,e^{i\phi}$, then:

• $R_{31}$ tells you $n=3$, $l=1$.

• The $e^{i\phi}$ gives $m=+1$.

• Therefore $|L|=\sqrt{2}\hbar$ and $L_z=+\hbar$.

• Radial nodes $= n-l-1 = 1$, angular nodes $= l = 1$.

Why the centrifugal term matters physically GOOD TO KNOW

The radial equation contains an effective repulsion: $$U_{\text{eff}}(r) = U(r) + \frac{l(l+1)\hbar^2}{2mr^2}$$ The second term gets bigger when $l$ is bigger, so large-$l$ states are pushed away from the origin. That is why s-states penetrate closest to the nucleus, p-states less, and d-states even less. This is exactly the idea used later to explain why 4s fills before 3d in multi-electron atoms.

Hydrogen-like Atoms (8.10) — One Electron, Nuclear Charge $Z$

Examples: He$^+$ ($Z=2$, 1 electron), Li$^{++}$ ($Z=3$, 1 electron). These are atoms with a single electron orbiting a nucleus with charge $+Ze$.

Everything scales with $Z$ MUST KNOW

Since the potential is still Coulomb ($U = -Zk_e e^2/r$), the hydrogen solution carries over with $e^2 \to Ze^2$:

$$\boxed{E_n = -\frac{Z^2 E_R}{n^2}}, \qquad \boxed{a_Z = \frac{a_B}{Z}}$$

Wavefunctions: replace $a_B \to a_B/Z$ everywhere:

$$\psi_{1s} = A\,e^{-Zr/a_B}, \qquad \psi_{2p} = A'\left(\frac{Zr}{a_B}\right)e^{-Zr/2a_B}$$

Charge densities are pulled $1/Z$ closer to the nucleus → stronger binding → higher ionization energy.

Angular wavefunctions $Y_{lm}(\theta,\phi)$ are completely unchanged — the $Z$ scaling only affects the radial part.

Why multi-electron atoms are different (leads to Ch 10) GOOD TO KNOW

For He (neutral, $Z=2$, 2 electrons), the potential includes electron-electron repulsion:

$$U_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{-\frac{Zk_e e^2}{r_1}}_{\text{e}_1\text{-nucleus}} + \underbrace{-\frac{Zk_e e^2}{r_2}}_{\text{e}_2\text{-nucleus}} + \underbrace{\frac{k_e e^2}{|\vec{r}_1-\vec{r}_2|}}_{\text{e}_1\text{-e}_2 \text{ repulsion}}$$

The last term depends on where both electrons are simultaneously — this makes the problem unsolvable exactly. That's why we need approximations like the IPA and Hartree-Fock.

Screening picture for Li ($Z = 3$) GOOD TO KNOW

Li has 3 electrons. The first two fill the $1s$ shell at radius $\sim a_B/3$ (inner shell).

The third electron (in $2s$) is much farther out. When it's at $r > a_B/3$, it "sees" the nucleus ($+3e$) screened by the two inner electrons ($-2e$), for a net charge of $+1e$. So $Z_{\text{eff}} \approx 1$ for the outer electron.

When the outer electron is at $r < a_B/3$ (inside the inner shell), it sees the full nuclear charge $Z_{\text{eff}} \approx 3$. This is why $s$-orbitals (which penetrate close to the nucleus) are lower in energy — they spend more time seeing $Z_{\text{eff}} > 1$.

Total Degeneracy of Hydrogen

Degeneracy including spin = $2n^2$ MUST KNOW

For a given $n$, the available states are: $l = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$, each with $m_l = -l, \ldots, +l$ (that's $2l+1$ values), and each with $m_s = \pm\frac{1}{2}$.

$$\text{Total states} = \sum_{l=0}^{n-1} 2(2l+1) = 2n^2$$

Example: $n=2$ → $l=0$ gives 2 states, $l=1$ gives 6 states → total $= 8 = 2(2^2)$.

You need all four quantum numbers ($n, l, m_l, m_s$) to completely specify a hydrogen state. Spatial probability distributions $|\psi_{nlm}|^2$ are not altered by $m_s$ — spin doesn't change where you find the electron.

Electron Spin & Pauli Exclusion

Statement: No two electrons in a quantum system can occupy the same state (i.e., share all quantum numbers).

This applies not just to electrons in atoms, but also to protons and neutrons in nuclei, and generally to all particles with half-integer spin.

Screening, $Z_{\text{eff}}$, and Why Multi-electron Atoms Are Harder Than Hydrogen

Hydrogen is simple because there is only one electron, so the potential is exactly Coulomb:

$$U(r) = -\frac{k_e e^2}{r}$$

In a multi-electron atom, each electron feels the nucleus and repulsion from the other electrons. So the true potential is not just a simple $1/r$ form anymore.

Independent-particle idea CONTEXT

We often approximate each electron as moving in an effective spherically symmetric potential:

$$U_{\text{eff}}(r) = -Z_{\text{eff}}(r)\frac{k_e e^2}{r}$$

Here $Z_{\text{eff}}(r)$ is the effective nuclear charge. Inner electrons screen the nucleus, so outer electrons feel less than the full charge $+Ze$.

Close to the nucleus, screening is weaker and $Z_{\text{eff}}(r)$ is larger. Farther out, screening is stronger and $Z_{\text{eff}}(r)$ is smaller.

What this changes compared with hydrogen CONTEXT

In hydrogen, energy depends only on $n$. In multi-electron atoms, energy depends on both $n$ and $l$ because different orbitals penetrate the electron cloud differently.

s-states penetrate closest to the nucleus, so they feel a larger $Z_{\text{eff}}$ and lie lower in energy than p-states with the same $n$. That is the physical reason behind $2s < 2p$, $3s < 3p < 3d$, and especially 4s below 3d.

Hartree-Fock Method CONTEXT

The Hartree-Fock method is a numerical/computer approach that treats each electron as feeling the influence of:

• The nuclear charge $+Ze$, and

• The $Z-1$ other electrons (not just their average position — it includes exchange effects from Pauli exclusion)

This is more accurate than the simple IPA below, but requires a computer to solve iteratively.

Independent Particle Approximation (IPA) — the exam-level version GOOD TO KNOW

The IPA is the simpler model: treat every electron as if it moves in its own effective single-particle potential:

$$\boxed{U_{\text{IPA}}(r) = -Z_{\text{eff}}(r)\frac{k_e e^2}{r}}$$

Key assumptions:

• All other electrons are spherically distributed around the nucleus

• An electron at small $r$ (close to nucleus) "sees" the full nuclear charge → $Z_{\text{eff}} \approx Z$

• An electron at large $r$ has $Z-1$ inner electrons screening $Z-1$ protons → $Z_{\text{eff}} \approx 1$

• $Z_{\text{eff}}(r)$ varies smoothly between these limits: $1 \le Z_{\text{eff}}(r) \le Z$

Energy levels in the IPA:

$$\boxed{E_n \approx -\frac{Z_{\text{eff}}^2}{n^2}\,E_R}$$

where $E_R = 13.6\,\text{eV}$ is the Rydberg energy (hydrogen ground state). This is just the hydrogen formula with $Z \to Z_{\text{eff}}$.

Physical takeaway: Screening breaks hydrogen's $l$-degeneracy and changes the filling order. Different $l$ values at the same $n$ have different $Z_{\text{eff}}$ because they penetrate the electron cloud differently.

Magnetic Moments and the Bohr Magneton

Classical derivation: why orbiting electrons have magnetic moments CONTEXT

A charged particle moving in a loop is a current loop with magnetic moment $\vec{\mu} = i\vec{A}$.

For an electron orbiting with speed $v$ at radius $r$:

• Current: $i = \dfrac{e}{T} = \dfrac{e}{2\pi r / v} = \dfrac{ev}{2\pi r}$

• Loop area: $A = \pi r^2$

• So $\mu = iA = \dfrac{ev}{2\pi r}\cdot\pi r^2 = \dfrac{1}{2}evr$

Since the classical angular momentum is $L = m_e v r$, we get:

$$\boxed{\frac{\mu}{L} = \frac{e}{2m_e}} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \vec{\mu}_{\text{orbital}} = -\frac{e}{2m_e}\vec{L}$$

The negative sign is because the electron has charge $-e$ (current flows opposite to electron motion).

Key point: the ratio $\mu/L = e/2m_e$ depends only on charge-to-mass ratio, not on the radius or speed — so it works quantum-mechanically too. Just replace $L$ with the quantized $L_z = m_l\hbar$.

Gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma$ GOOD TO KNOW

The gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma$ relates the magnetic moment to angular momentum: $\vec{\mu} = -\gamma\vec{L}$.

• For orbital motion: $\gamma_{\text{orb}} = \dfrac{e}{2m_e}$

• For spin: $\gamma_{\text{spin}} \approx \dfrac{e}{m_e}$ (about twice the orbital value!)

The factor-of-2 difference for spin is what makes the Zeeman effect "anomalous." Just memorize: orbital gives $e/2m_e$, spin gives $e/m_e$.

Orbital magnetic moment
$$\vec{\mu}_L = -\frac{e}{2m_e}\vec{L}$$

Negative sign because electron charge is $-e$. Uses $L_z = m_l\hbar$.

Spin magnetic moment (factor of 2!)
$$\vec{\mu}_S \approx -\frac{e}{m_e}\vec{S}$$

Note: the coefficient is $e/m_e$, twice the orbital value $e/2m_e$. This is the "anomalous" magnetic moment.

Bohr magneton $\mu_B$ MUST KNOW
$$\boxed{\mu_B = \frac{e\hbar}{2m_e} \approx 9.27 \times 10^{-24}\,\text{J/T} \approx 5.79 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{eV/T}}$$

This is the natural unit for atomic magnetic moments. It appears everywhere in Zeeman splitting.

In terms of $\mu_B$:   $\mu_{L,z} = -\mu_B m_l$   and   $\mu_{S,z} \approx -2\mu_B m_s$

Total atomic magnetic moment MUST KNOW

Combining orbital and spin contributions:

$$\boxed{\vec{\mu}_{\text{atom}} = -\frac{e}{2m_e}(\vec{L}+2\vec{S})}$$

The total angular momentum is $\vec{J} = \vec{L} + \vec{S}$, but $\vec{\mu}$ is not simply proportional to $\vec{J}$ because of the factor of 2 in front of $\vec{S}$. This is the fundamental reason spin complicates atomic spectra.

Normal Zeeman Effect: Orbital Splitting In A Magnetic Field

A magnetic moment in a magnetic field has potential energy:

$$\Delta E = -\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B}$$

The $\vec{B}$ field defines the $z$-axis. Take $\vec{B} = B_z\hat{z}$. Then using $\mu_{L,z} = -\frac{e}{2m_e}L_z$ with $L_z = m_l\hbar$:

Normal Zeeman energy shift MUST KNOW
$$\boxed{\Delta E = \mu_B\,m_l\,B_z}$$

A level with orbital quantum number $l$ splits into $2l+1$ sublevels, one for each $m_l=-l,\ldots,+l$.

Adjacent Zeeman levels are separated by:

$$\boxed{\Delta E_{\text{adjacent}} = \mu_B B}$$

$m_l$ is often called the "magnetic quantum number" — this is exactly the reason: it labels the energy shifts in a magnetic field.

Fig 9.2 — Normal Zeeman Effect. A 2p level (l=1) splits into 3 sublevels in a magnetic field, one for each m_l value. Adjacent levels are separated by μ_B·B. This is the "normal" Zeeman effect — orbital angular momentum only, no spin.
Helium example (He with 2p electron) GOOD TO KNOW

He atom: 1st electron in $1s$ ($l=0$, $\vec{\mu}_L=0$), 2nd electron promoted to $2p$ ($l=1$).

At $B=0$: the energy is $E_n \sim Z_{\text{eff}}^2 E_R/n^2$. For $n=2$: $E_{2,2p} \approx -21\,\text{eV}$.

At $B \neq 0$: the $2p$ level splits into $m_l = -1, 0, +1$ with shifts $\pm\mu_B B$ and $0$.

A single spectral line from the $2p \to 1s$ transition now becomes three closely spaced lines — the normal Zeeman triplet.

Anomalous Zeeman Effect: When Spin Changes Everything

Why it is called "anomalous" GOOD TO KNOW

The normal Zeeman effect only uses orbital angular momentum and gives $2l+1$ equally spaced levels. But real electrons also have spin, and spin's magnetic moment is twice as strong per unit angular momentum ($\gamma_{\text{spin}} = 2\gamma_{\text{orb}}$). This changes both the number of split levels and the size of the splitting.

Spin-only splitting: ground state of hydrogen ($l = 0$) MUST KNOW

Consider H ground state: $n=1$, $l=0$, so $\vec{\mu}_L = 0$. Only spin contributes:

$$\vec{\mu}_{\text{atom}} = \vec{\mu}_S = -\frac{e}{m_e}\vec{S}$$

In a field $B\hat{z}$, with $S_z = m_s\hbar$ and $m_s = \pm\frac12$:

$$\boxed{\Delta E = 2\mu_B m_s B = \pm\mu_B B}$$

This gives two levels separated by $2\mu_B B$.

Fig 9.3 — Anomalous Zeeman Effect. For an l=0 state, only spin contributes. The factor-of-2 enhancement of the spin magnetic moment (γ_spin = 2γ_orb) means the splitting is 2μ_B·B instead of the "normal" μ_B·B.

Compare with atomic energy scales: $E_{1s}^{\text{He}} = -13.6\,\text{eV}$, $E_{2p}^{\text{He}} \approx -3.4\,\text{eV}$, $1s\to2p$ gap $\approx 21\,\text{eV}$.

Step 1: What kind of potential?

→ Infinite well → $\psi_n = \sqrt{2/a}\sin(n\pi x/a)$, $E_n = n^2 E_0$

→ 1D: solve directly

Common Exam Traps

Watch out for these MUST KNOW

1. $T \neq |B/A_0|^2$ — don't forget the $k_2/k_1$ factor in transmission.

2. $|\vec{L}| = \sqrt{l(l+1)}\hbar$, NOT $l\hbar$.

3. $l$ goes from $0$ to $n-1$, not $n$.

4. Radial probability is $P(r) = r^2|R|^2$, not $|R|^2$.

5. 4s fills before 3d in multi-electron atoms.

6. Dimensions of $\psi$: 1D: $1/\sqrt{\text{m}}$; 3D: $1/\text{m}^{3/2}$.

7. Degeneracy: Don't forget spin (×2) when counting electron states.
